Product Concept · v1 · macOS & Linux · single user

A quiet, minimal time tracker for one person who bills by the hour — where the tray and the terminal are equal citizens of the same local database, and every tracked hour is one you can trust on an invoice.

01 The idea

One person, honest hours, two front doors

Stint is a desktop time tracker in the spirit of old Toggl: start a timer, do the work, stop it. It runs entirely on your own computer, stores everything in a single local SQLite file, and never touches the network.

It exists to answer one question accurately — how much time did I spend, and for whom — so you can bill from it with a clear conscience. It deliberately stops there. Stint deals only in time; rates, currency, tax, and the invoice itself live in whatever tool you already use. Its job is to hand that tool a number it can stand behind.

Two things make it more than a stopwatch: it treats the command line as a first-class way in, not an afterthought, and it works quietly to keep your hours honest — catching the timer you left running overnight, flagging time the machine slept through, and never silently rounding a number up or down behind your back.

02 The insight

A running timer is just an open row

The hardest part of "the terminal and the window both control the live timer" sounds like a synchronization problem. It isn't, once you see it the right way.

There is no timer object ticking somewhere. A running timer is simply the one time entry whose end time is still empty. "Running" is a row, not a process.

Everything follows from that. Elapsed time is never stored or incremented — it's derived, now − start, by whoever happens to be looking. The tray counting up each second is pure display; close the lid for an hour and reopen it and the number is simply correct, because it was never being kept anywhere to drift. And because the live timer is just data in the shared file, the terminal and the window aren't two systems that must agree — they're two views of one truth. Start a timer with tt start and the tray reflects it almost instantly; stop it from the tray and tt status sees it stopped. Neither is in charge. Neither needs the other running.

That single reframing is the backbone of the whole product. It's why the CLI can have full parity with the GUI without a second copy of the logic, and why "both first-class citizens" is structural rather than a promise. The architecture that follows from it — one core, one file, two surfaces — is laid out in architecture.html.

03 Who it's for

The solo professional who invoices by time

Stint is built for one specific person: a freelancer, consultant, or contractor working solo at their own computer, who tracks time across a handful of clients and turns those hours into invoices elsewhere. They value a clean, fast, unobtrusive tool, and many of them are comfortable in a terminal — comfortable enough to want the timer there too.

It is not built for teams, managers, agencies needing dashboards, or anyone who wants the tracker itself to produce invoices. Saying no to those keeps it small and sharp.

04 A day with Stint

Ambient, then out of the way

You sit down, hit Ctrl+Alt+T or type a line in the terminal, and you're tracking. The timer lives in the tray — present, countable at a glance, never a window in your face. When you switch from one client to another you start a new timer; the old one closes itself. There's only ever one running, the way there's only ever one thing you're actually doing.

The work you do most often you shouldn't have to retype. The clients and projects you live in become favorites — a pinned timer you can resume in one click, description and tags already filled in, so the morning's first stint is a single tap rather than a small form.

$ tt start "auth refactor" --client "client a" --project api
▸ started · client a / api · 09:14

$ tt status
▸ running 01:24:07 · "auth refactor" · client a / api

$ tt stop
■ stopped · 1h 24m · client a / api

An hour in, a gentle notification asks whether you're still on this task, and offers to push the next nudge out as far as you like. Forget to stop before lunch and walk away, and Stint quietly notices the machine slept — it flags those minutes so they never reach an invoice unnoticed, and offers to subtract them with one tap. Forgot to start the timer at all? Add the block by hand after the fact; backfilled time is a first-class entry, not a second-class apology.

At the end of the month you don't rebuild the same report from scratch. The one you run every cycle — this client, this range, grouped the way you like, rounded the way you bill — is saved by name; you open it, eyeball the total, and export it as CSV or JSON. That file is the bridge to your invoicing tool. Stint's part is done.

05 Two equal front doors

Tray and terminal, one database

The tray

Home base. The running timer, always visible and one click from start, stop, or switch. A main window opens for the day's entries, edits, clients, and reports.

The terminal

Full parity. tt can do everything the window can — track, backfill, edit, split, merge, manage clients, run and export reports — with human-readable tables by default and --json when you're scripting.

Both speak to the same SQLite file through the same shared core, so they can't disagree about what's true. When one writes, the other notices within a heartbeat by watching the file. The classic worry — "won't the GUI and CLI fight over the live timer?" — never materializes, because the live timer is just a row and the core enforces the one rule that matters: at most one entry is open at a time.

Both arrive together, too. Stint installs in one step: the app lands in your Applications folder or app launcher and tt lands on your PATH at the same time — no separate setup for the terminal half. And because the whole project lives in a public GitHub repository where every merge to main is a new release, Stint can quietly keep itself current, pulling the latest published version without ever asking you to go hunting for a download.

06 Honest hours

The number you can stand behind

For a billing tool, the cardinal sin is a total that's quietly wrong. Stint takes a firm position: store the exact truth, and never mutate it silently.

  • Rounding is a lens, not an edit. Every entry is stored to the second. Rounding to tidy quarter-hours happens only when a report is shown or exported, only if you turn it on, and it rounds the billable line — not each entry, which is how trackers accidentally inflate invoices.
  • Sleep is recorded, not assumed. The operating system hands over timestamped sleep and wake events through its power monitor. Stint records each span the machine slept through a running timer and flags the entry. Whether that time counts is your call, made with one tap — never auto-deleted, never auto-kept.
  • Overlaps are surfaced, not buried. Backfilling can accidentally double-book an hour. Stint warns you, lets it through if you meant it, and marks overlapped time in reports so it can't silently land on two invoices.
  • Check-ins guard the left-running timer. An autonomous nudge after the first hour — and at a steady interval after — so a timer you forgot doesn't turn into a day you can't bill.

Notably, Stint does not watch whether you're idle at the keyboard. That signal is fuzzy, OS-dependent, and really a job for the operating system, not a time tracker. Sleep and wake are clean and authoritative; idle is not. Stint builds only on the solid ground.

07 The look

Monochrome and quiet

Stint is a quiet, minimal desktop app for macOS and Linux that tries to disappear into your system — system typography, a real tray presence, and a window that follows your light and dark mode automatically. The palette is restrained to system grays; it borrows your own system accent color for the single primary action and the running state, and nothing else. The running time is the one thing that gets to be big.

The discipline is the point. A minimal direction earns its elegance in spacing, weight, and detail rather than ornament. Stint should feel like a well-made instrument: quiet at rest, legible in motion, nothing on screen that isn't doing a job.

08 What Stint is not

The edges, on purpose

no money

No rates, currency, tax, or invoice generation. The unit is time; billing happens downstream.

no cloud

Fully offline. No accounts, no sync, no network, no telemetry. One file on your own computer.

no team

Single user, single machine. No sharing, no roles, no dashboards.

no idle policing

Sleep and wake, yes. Watching your keyboard for inactivity, no — that's the OS's job.

no mobile

A desktop instrument for desktop work.

09 The name

Stint

A stint is a bounded spell of work — exactly what each tracked entry is. It sits naturally beside the product's own vocabulary of spans and sessions, reads quietly in lowercase to match the monochrome feel, and leaves the command line its two-letter shorthand: tt.
